Requesting a Veterinary Team: Key Responsibilities

It is a core requirement that international animal groups and individuals secure local resources when requesting a veterinary team. Support from the local government and community is highly valued. These factors make a project more desirable, help the traveling surgery team develop new projects more efficiently, and are often crucial in determining project feasibility.

Legality & Licenses

International animal groups and individuals are responsible for investigating their government's policy regarding foreign veterinarians volunteering services within the country and assisting with obtaining such licenses. They are also required to secure any necessary licenses and/or permits required by law to import veterinary supplies.

Safety

Safety is the number one priority for our veterinary teams. We do not plan projects in areas that are at war or have a high threat of terrorist activity. We consult the US State Department travel advisory website for all project locations and, as a general rule, do not plan projects in areas with a travel warning.

Local Veterinary Community

Local partners need to establish whether a local veterinarian and/or individual practicing veterinary medicine is available in the area. Local veterinarians are expected to be supportive of a traveling surgery team program. The traveling surgery team must complement and enhance available veterinary resources, not compete with them. Surgical complications will need to be sent to them for treatment so their cooperation is vital. The cost of treatment for any complications must be borne by the pet owner or the local partner.

Operating Budget

The traveling surgery team must evaluate and assess all project expenses to determine financial feasibility. Local partners need to establish their own operating budget for a surgery clinic as they are responsible for providing local resources. Local partners should actively seek donations from within their community. Monetary donations received during the clinic will be given to the local coordinating group.

Language

The traveling surgery team members only speak English. Local partners are expected to ensure at least one local bilingual volunteer is present during a traveling surgery team clinic.

Ongoing Requirements & Logistics

Controlled Drugs

Local partners are required to provide controlled drugs, such as ketamine and valium. Purchasing these locally is a necessity. Exporting these drugs from the United States has become difficult and costly.

Accessibility

Local partners need to confirm that the requested location is easily accessible for a veterinary team. They are responsible for providing information on the time and logistical requirements necessary to arrive at the final destination.

Time of Year

Weather plays a significant role in project success. We will avoid the rainy season in many countries, as bad weather complicates logistics and hinders clinic work days. The traveling surgery team will not schedule a project around the time when local or national elections are being held. We also refrain from planning projects during national holidays and major celebrations observed in the host country. This reduces attendance at clinics and makes travel challenging.

Local Activities

Local partners are responsible for assisting in organizing activities for the veterinary team during their leisure time (roughly 2â€“3 days). Local partners are required to provide a list of activities ahead of time so the veterinary team can anticipate extracurricular activities.

Required Local Provisions

Many supplies must be provided locally by international animal groups to ensure the successful execution of a traveling surgery team clinic:

Building/Room

  • For a small animal project, the ideal space is at least 900 sq ft or a building with a couple of adjacent rooms providing that much space.
  • Access to electricity and running water and bathroom facilities is a necessity. If not available, local partners are responsible for identifying and securing alternatives nearby for the veterinary team to utilize.

Local Transportation

Local partners must coordinate local transportation prior to the veterinary team's arrival for:

  • Airport Transfers (to and from).
  • Clinic Work Days (to and from the clinic location, if not walkable).

The ideal local transportation is a van/microbus with a capacity for the entire team. Local partners should seek donations of transportation from community members or local businesses. If someone from the team can drive locally this is ideal.

Lunches and Drinks

During clinic work days, local partners are responsible for providing the veterinary team with drinks (e.g., potable drinking water suitable for consumption by non-locals and/or soda) as well as breakfast (if not provided by accommodation) and lunch for the entire team.

Safety Requirement: Local partners must be certain the meals are coming from a sanitary source, as we have experienced severe food poisoning incidents in the past. This can easily halt operations resulting in failure of the project.

Recommended Source: It is recommended that these meals be provided by local restaurants and that you have those restaurants donate and deliver the food. In exchange you will take photos of the team with their food and any banners/etc they would like to provide and post on social media promoting their business. Also ask them to promote the clinic in their place of business and with their contacts to increase patient numbers.

Accommodations

Local partners need to provide a place for the team to stay. A vacation house or condo rental is preferred in an area near the clinic and in a "tourist" area within walking distance of a grocery and restaurants/bars.

  • Dog kennels and/or animal cages

Local Volunteers

Local partners need to secure local volunteers for clinic work days for the following activities:

  • Patient Check-In
  • Transport animals to and from the clinic
  • Translators
  • Publicize/promote the veterinary services
  • Instrument Cleaning
  • Animal Recovery and Discharge

Advertising

Local partners are responsible for advertising and promoting the veterinary services to be provided before the veterinary team arrives. This is essential to ensure a good turnout. Suggested media outlets include newspaper, radio, flyers, door-to-door, public address loudspeaker cars, as well as any other effective communication method used in your particular community.

Activities (Optional/Not required)

We appreciate any discounts and/or donations of fun activities for the team by local businesses. Examples include boat day cruises, ATV tours, zip lines, etc.

Preparing for a Veterinary Team: Education & Raising Awareness

A primary component of any traveling surgery team project is education and raising awareness. Since a traveling surgery team is only present for a limited amount of time, this responsibility primarily rests with the local operating animal group or individual coordinating the visit.

Local partners should utilize community members who take advantage of the free services as advocates. These individuals will experience the positive effects firsthand and will spread the word.

Local partners should appeal to community members by relating animal health and wellbeing issues to their own lives, pointing out the benefits for them and the overall improvement of their community. (See talking points on the benefits of spay/neuter).

Local partners should run an advertising campaign for 3â€“4 weeks prior to the visit. The key to success is maintaining the issue of animal health on the minds of the community even when veterinary teams are not present.

Harnessing & Maintaining Local Support

Local support is critical for determining both the feasibility and success of a project. Education and raising awareness should be used to capture local support. Local partners need to recognize the community members and/or businesses that volunteer or donate resources through social media posts and other venues.

Starter Checklist for Local Partners

Project & Planning

  • Veterinary Aid Required: Small animal (spay/neuter and health consultations), Equine, Livestock, Ruminants, Wildlife, Other Needs.
  • Accommodations: Is decent, secure lodging available to house the entire group?
  • Budget: Local partners are responsible for calculating the necessary funds and need to identify all items that can be secured through local donations.
  • Legal Paperwork: Local partners are required to confirm and file any legal paperwork for volunteer vets and/or veterinary supplies.
  • Clinic Location: The local partners need to confirm the building/room meets the following: Enough space (min. 900 sq ft for small animal), Electricity, Water, Working toilet, Security.

Logistics & Support Checklist

  • Transportation: Is required to be coordinated for: Airport (arrival/departure) and During the clinic days.
  • Food and Drinks: Local partners are responsible for ensuring the veterinary team has breakfast, lunch, and drinks on clinic days.
  • Patient Check In Sheets: Local partners need to ensure enough paper and access to a printer/photocopy machine to make about 150 copies.
  • Local Supplies to Provide: 5â€“7 tables, 1L H2O2, 3â€“4L Isopropyl Alcohol, paper towels, garbage bags, cleaning spray, dish tubs, fans, tarps/sheets, and dog kennels/cages.
  • Volunteers to Secure: Patient check in, Transporting animals, Translator(s), and Organizing activities.
  • Advertising: Local partners are responsible for developing an effective advertising strategy to ensure everyone in town knows when and where.

Clinic Work Stations & Procedures

Designate a "Clinic Manager"

The designated Clinic Manager is accountable for the following core responsibilities:

  • Act as the main contact for the traveling surgery team and the local community.
  • Direct and supervise all local volunteers.
  • Oversee and manage all animals entering and leaving the clinic.
  • Communicate with Patient Check In and Recovery regarding patient flow and release.
  • Communicate with pet owners about the surgery process and take-home aftercare instructions.
  • Organize and ensure local support (food/drinks, transportation, supplies) is present during the clinic days.

Patient Check In (Reception)

This station is required to be located at the entrance of the clinic (table, chairs, and local volunteers needed). Their responsibilities include:

  • Greet and register each pet owner.
  • Fill out 2 separate forms: general roster and surgery form.
  • Ask about any previous health concerns.
  • Inform and provide written and spoken take-home instructions.
  • Inform the "Clinic Manager" of waiting patients and when owners return.

Veterinary Service Stations

Stations include Pre-Med, Surgery, Cleaning and sterilizing instruments, Recovery, and Medical Consultations. Local veterinarians and students are encouraged to participate.

Opportunities for local vets/vet students to practice surgery will be determined by the team based on the specific clinic; it is not standard practice to allow local veterinary students to perform surgery during their first clinic participation.

After a Veterinary Team Visit

Aftercare

Local partners need to ensure that pet owners are given both written and spoken instructions. Local partners should visit certain animals, make house calls, and call pet owners to check on recovery. The veterinary team is typically available for 1 day after the clinic for follow-up.

Evaluation & Feedback

Both local partners and the traveling surgery team should evaluate all project aspects. Local partners are expected to share the clinic results, including the number of animals and types of cases seen, as well as photos.

Planning Next Visit

Planning the next visit is done with a designated staff member and should incorporate all feedback from the evaluation. Follow-up visits may be canceled if the traveling surgery team faces insurmountable challenges (logistics, local authorities, local partner relations, and/or unforeseen difficulties).

After Surgery Instructions (For Pet Owners)

Your pet has just undergone an operation with anesthesia. It is critical to take proper care of your pet for a minimum of 1 week following surgery.

Section Instructions
WATER & FOOD Anesthesia can cause an upset stomach. You must restrict food for the first 6 hours. You must gradually increase their food and water each day.
BATHE/GET WET Do not let your pet get wet or bathe them for a minimum of 7 days after surgery.
PHYSICAL ACTIVITY You must restrict physical activity for 7 days. Dogs must be kept on a leash. Cats must be put in a small, confined area.
MONITOR THE INCISION You must examine the incision twice a day. If excessive bleeding/inflammation occurs, you must contact a veterinarian. Do not let your pet lick the incision. The dissolvable suture does not need to be removed.
MEDICINE Do not give Aspirin or Tylenol unless specifically prescribed. Take-home pain medication can be obtained from the veterinary team.
